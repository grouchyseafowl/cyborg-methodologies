## Core Question
How does this text reproduce, naturalize, or challenge social power relations?

## What This Framework Sees
How discourse reproduces or challenges power structures through the interaction of textual features, discursive practices, and social conditions. Naturalization of ideology — the process by which historically contingent arrangements come to appear as common sense. The relationship between specific grammatical choices and broader social formations. Ideological work accomplished through what a text includes, excludes, or renders unthinkable.

## What This Framework Misses
Individual agency within discourse — speakers and writers navigating structural constraints in ways that exceed structural determination. Positive and constructive discursive work (Martin's critique: CDA is better at finding what's wrong than what's right). Micro-level interactional dynamics and sequential organization (conversation analysis handles this). May over-determine structural explanations by under-attending to the messiness of actual discursive practice.

## Not For
Simple readability or style assessment. Purely aesthetic or literary analysis without a power dimension. Analyzing texts where the relevant social structure is unknown or where the analyst lacks the contextual knowledge to specify what social practice the discourse is embedded in — Dimension 3 requires that knowledge; without it, you have a textual analysis dressed up as CDA.

## Analytical Protocol

Work through the three dimensions in sequence, then synthesize across them. The power of this model is the connection between levels — don't stop at Dimension 1.

### Dimension 1: Text Analysis

**Transitivity** (realize this via SFL; see sfl.md for full protocol):
- Identify process type distribution for analytically significant clauses. What process types dominate?
- Track agency: Who acts? Who is acted upon? Who is beneficiary?
- Where is agency deleted? Look for:
  - Passive without by-phrase: "The decision was made" — deletes the decision-maker
  - Nominalization: "the implementation of reforms" — converts an action (someone implements) into a thing, burying the agent
  - Existential processes: "There are problems" — the problems simply exist, not produced by anyone

**Modality**:
- Map the modal landscape: what's asserted without hedging (categorical), what's hedged (epistemic modality), what's obligated (deontic)?
- High deontic modality (must, shall, required) — who is obligated to whom?
- Where the text speaks with high certainty vs. hedges: what does that pattern reveal about the text's authority claims?

**Theme/Rheme**:
- What is foregrounded in thematic position as "given" — already established, not requiring justification?
- Marked themes (non-default fronting) signal rhetorical choices worth attention.
- Thematic progression: does the text build from point to point, or circle a fixed center?

**Cohesion and Presupposition**:
- Lexical cohesion: what semantic fields are constructed through repeated and collocated terms? What is grouped together?
- Logical connectors: what relations does the text claim (causation, concession, condition)? Are these relations earned or asserted?
- Presupposition: what does the text treat as background, not requiring argument?
  - Existential presuppositions: "the problem of X" presupposes X is a problem
  - Factive presuppositions: "we know that X" presupposes X is true
  - Structural presuppositions embedded in questions: "why do immigrants fail to integrate?" presupposes they do
- Flag presuppositions that do ideological work — they are often where naturalization happens

### Dimension 2: Discursive Practice Analysis

**Production**:
- Who produced this text? Under what institutional conditions and constraints?
- What genres and discourse types were drawn on? Academic report? Legal document? News? Policy brief?
- Interdiscursivity: where do different discourse types mix? What is the significance of that mixing? (e.g., medical discourse colonizing education policy)

**Distribution**:
- What channels carry this text? To whom? With what gatekeeping?
- Is this a text that circulates widely or is restricted? What does that say about who it is meant to constitute as audience?

**Consumption**:
- What reading positions does the text construct? What kind of reader does it interpellate?
- What prior knowledge, values, or subject positions does the text assume?
- Who can read against the grain? Who is positioned to read with it?

**Intertextuality**:
- What texts are explicitly cited, alluded to, or assumed? What is the chain of intertextual reference?
- What voices are incorporated (direct and indirect)? Whose is absent?
- What discourses are presupposed as shared knowledge?

### Dimension 3: Social Practice Analysis

This is where the analysis becomes CDA rather than just close reading. Connect your textual and discursive findings to social structure.

- What social relations (class, race, gender, professional hierarchy, institution/citizen) does this discourse help reproduce or challenge?
- What ideological work does the text accomplish? What interests does it serve?
- What is naturalized — presented as common sense, inevitable, or beyond dispute? Trace this back to specific textual features identified in Dimension 1.
- What alternatives are excluded, marginalized, or rendered unthinkable? What would a different discursive construction make available?
- What are the material consequences of this discursive construction? Who benefits? Who is harmed?

### Synthesis Move

After working through all three dimensions:
- Connect at least two textual features from Dimension 1 to the discursive practice account in Dimension 2, and then to the social practice analysis in Dimension 3.
- Don't leave the analysis at Dimension 1 with a gestural reference to "power" — the three-way connection is the argument.

## Output Emphasis

- Name what is naturalized — not just "the text assumes X" but "the text treats X as common sense by placing it in thematic position / by nominalization / by presupposition, which makes X unavailable for argument."
- Name what is excluded or rendered unthinkable — absence is as analytically significant as presence.
- Connect textual features to social practice through the discursive practice level — the three dimensions are analytical lenses, not separate reports.
- CDA analyzes the effects of discourse on social practice — never claim "the author deliberately..." or "the text intends to..." Attribution of intent is not available from textual analysis; effects and functions are.
- When Fairclough is combined with van-Leeuwen legitimation or Wodak DHA, use those frameworks to extend Dimension 2/3 analysis; use SFL to extend Dimension 1 precision. The combination points should be explicit in the output.
