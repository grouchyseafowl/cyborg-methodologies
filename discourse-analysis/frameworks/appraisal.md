## Core Question
How does this text evaluate, adopt stances, and negotiate solidarity?

## What This Framework Sees
The evaluative language system in its full complexity: what attitudes are expressed (Affect, Judgment, Appreciation), how they are amplified or softened (Graduation), and how the text positions itself within a field of voices (Engagement). Reveals the evaluative architecture of a text — the pattern of who and what is assessed, on what grounds, and with what force. Captures evaluative work that thematic or content coding would flatten into sentiment or miss entirely, particularly invoked appraisal that triggers evaluation without explicit evaluative lexis.

## What This Framework Misses
Ideational meaning — what the text is about (SFL transitivity handles this). Textual organization — how the text is sequenced and structured (SFL theme/rheme handles this). Social and political context — whose values the evaluations serve (Fairclough Dimension 3 handles this). Narrative structure (Labov). Community-specific evaluative norms without which invoked appraisal cannot be reliably interpreted — the framework provides the analytical categories; the analyst supplies the cultural knowledge to apply them.

## Not For
Sentiment analysis — Appraisal is categorically distinct from positive/negative valence scoring. The same clause may carry negative Affect, positive Judgment (of capacity), and negative Appreciation (of composition) simultaneously. Flattening to sentiment loses the framework's analytical value. Texts where evaluation is entirely implicit and where the analyst lacks sufficient domain and community knowledge to interpret invoked appraisal — flag this limitation rather than over-coding.

## Analytical Protocol

Work through the three systems (Attitude, Graduation, Engagement) for analytically significant passages. For each Attitude instance, record: system (Affect/Judgment/Appreciation), sub-type, polarity, and whether inscribed or invoked.

### System 1: ATTITUDE — What Is Being Evaluated and How

#### Affect: Emotional Responses
Evaluations of emotional states — the text's representation of feelings (its own, another's, or a projected reader's).

**Sub-types by emotional domain**:
- Happiness / unhappiness: emotions on the happy-sad axis — joy, love, pleasure / grief, hate, misery
- Security / insecurity: emotions on the confident-anxious axis — confidence, trust, comfort / fear, anxiety, disquiet
- Satisfaction / dissatisfaction: emotions on the fulfilled-frustrated axis — desire, interest, wonder / tedium, ennui, exasperation

**Polarity**: positive (happy, secure, satisfied) or negative (unhappy, insecure, dissatisfied).

**Inscribed vs. invoked** (see critical note below):
- Inscribed Affect: explicit emotional lexis — "I was devastated," "they felt hopeful," "a joyful occasion"
- Invoked Affect: ideational content that triggers an emotional reading without explicit emotional lexis — "after the third rejection, she returned to the empty apartment" invokes negative Affect without naming it

#### Judgment: Evaluation of Human Behavior
Evaluations of the behavior of human actors — what they do, how they do it, who they are.

**Social esteem** (community norms — infringement does not imply moral condemnation):
- Normality: how usual is this behavior? typical / unusual, eccentric, deviant
- Capacity: how capable is this actor? competent, skilled / incompetent, clumsy
- Tenacity: how resolute? persistent, reliable / irresolute, undependable

**Social sanction** (codified norms — infringement implies moral condemnation):
- Veracity: how truthful? honest, credible / dishonest, deceptive
- Propriety: how ethical? good, moral, law-abiding / corrupt, immoral, criminal

**Polarity**: positive (praised behavior) or negative (censured behavior).

**Pattern to look for**: the distribution of Judgment sub-types reveals the grounds on which the text morally evaluates. A text that consistently judges group A on veracity (dishonest, unreliable) and group B on capacity (skilled, effective) is constructing an asymmetric evaluative frame. Identify which actors receive which grounds for judgment.

#### Appreciation: Evaluation of Things, Processes, States of Affairs
Evaluations of objects, texts, artifacts, events, and states of affairs (not human behavior as such).

**Reaction** (the text's response to something):
- Impact (did it grab me?): arresting, compelling / dull, boring
- Quality (did I like it?): beautiful, elegant, pleasing / ugly, unpleasant

**Composition** (how the thing is put together):
- Balance (did it hang together?): unified, balanced / contradictory, incoherent
- Complexity (how complex?): elaborate, intricate / simplistic, accessible

**Valuation** (what is it worth?):
- Significant, innovative, valuable, important / insignificant, redundant, worthless

**Polarity**: positive or negative.

**Note**: Appreciation can become covert Judgment when directed at artifacts that stand in for their human creators — "the policy is incoherent" (Appreciation: composition) also evaluates the capacity of its authors. Track these moves.

### System 2: GRADUATION — How Attitude Is Amplified or Softened

Graduation operates on Attitude. Every attitude expression has a graduation value — it is not optional.

#### Force: The Intensity of the Evaluation
- **Raise** (amplify): intensifiers ("very," "extremely," "utterly," "absolutely"), repetition, mass quantification ("many," "all," "every"), emphatic rhythm
- **Lower** (diminish): softeners ("somewhat," "slightly," "a bit," "rather"), restrictive quantification ("few," "some," "limited"), hedged approximation

#### Focus: The Sharpness of the Category Boundaries
- **Sharpen**: "a true friend," "a real disaster," "a genuine contribution" — the modifier asserts full category membership
- **Soften**: "sort of," "kind of," "a type of," "something like a..." — the modifier loosens the category boundary, partial membership

**Pattern to look for**: asymmetric graduation. If positive evaluations are consistently amplified (very excellent, truly remarkable) and negative evaluations are consistently softened (somewhat concerning, perhaps a little inconsistent), or vice versa, the graduation pattern is doing rhetorical work independent of the attitude. The text says something is bad; the graduation says how bad it dares to say it is.

### System 3: ENGAGEMENT — How the Text Positions Among Other Voices

Engagement describes the dialogic positioning of the text — how it acknowledges, incorporates, or suppresses other voices and alternative positions.

#### Monogloss
A bare assertion with no reference to other voices, no modalization, no attribution. "The policy is effective." Presented as categorical, not arguable, shared. High monogloss in a text = high discursive authority claimed for those propositions. The text speaks without hedging, without attribution, without acknowledging that others might say otherwise.

#### Heterogloss — Dialogic Expansion (opening space for alternatives)

**Entertain**: the text acknowledges that the proposition is one perspective among possible others.
- Modal-based: "may," "might," "could," "possibly," "perhaps," "it seems"
- Function: marks the claim as provisional, not categorical. Opens dialogic space.

**Attribute**: the proposition is attributed to an external source.
- "According to X," "X argues that," "X suggests," "in X's view"
- Function: creates dialogic distance. The attributed proposition is voiced but not necessarily endorsed.
- Note: attribution is not endorsement — "X claims that..." is distancing; "X demonstrates that..." is endorsing (Proclaim-endorse).

#### Heterogloss — Dialogic Contraction (closing down alternatives)

**Disclaim**:
- Deny: "It is not the case that," "contrary to [position], X" — explicitly negates a position
- Counter: "Although X, actually Y" / "while it may seem that X, in fact Y" — acknowledges and overrides

**Proclaim**:
- Concur: "Of course," "naturally," "as everyone knows," "obviously" — presents proposition as shared, common ground
- Pronounce: "The fact is," "indeed," "the truth is," "I contend that" — explicit authorial intervention to assert
- Endorse: "Research demonstrates," "X proves," "the data confirm" — attributes to external source but presents as fully reliable, closing the dialogic space

**Key distinction**: Attribute (expansion) vs. Proclaim-endorse (contraction). "X argues that Y" — dialogic space maintained. "X demonstrates that Y" / "As X has shown, Y" — dialogic space closed. The verb of attribution carries the graduation.

**Pattern to look for**: the monogloss/heterogloss map of the text. Where does the text speak without qualification? Where does it hedge? Where does it invoke shared knowledge? The transitions between monogloss and heterogloss zones often mark the most analytically significant sites — what the text treats as beyond argument, what it concedes is debatable, what it suppresses by speaking as if there is no alternative.

### CRITICAL NOTE: Inscribed vs. Invoked Appraisal

This distinction is not optional. Conflating them produces unreliable analysis.

**Inscribed appraisal**: explicit evaluative lexis. "Terrible," "brilliant," "dishonest," "innovative." Code with high confidence.

**Invoked appraisal**: evaluation triggered by ideational content — by what is described, not by explicit evaluative markers.
- "The committee met for the third time without reaching a decision" — invokes negative appreciation (composition: coherence) and possibly negative judgment (capacity) without any evaluative word.
- "After the vote, she left without speaking" — the ideational framing invokes affect; which affect depends on context.

**Protocol for invoked appraisal**:
1. Flag it as invoked rather than inscribed.
2. State the evaluative reading you're proposing.
3. State your confidence level: HIGH (context strongly constrains the reading), MEDIUM (plausible but requires cultural knowledge), LOW (genuinely ambiguous).
4. For MEDIUM or LOW confidence: present both or all possible readings rather than silently resolving the ambiguity.
5. Check the project context for prior cultural coding decisions — if this is an ongoing research project with established community frames, invoke those. Do not silently apply default cultural assumptions.

## Output Emphasis

**Attitude patterns**: Report what is evaluated positively and negatively, and on what grounds. Who/what receives Affect? Who/what receives Judgment? On which Judgment sub-types? The pattern of grounds (capacity vs. veracity vs. propriety; reaction vs. valuation) reveals the evaluative framework the text operates within, not just the evaluative verdicts.

**Graduation patterns**: Report where evaluation is amplified and where softened. Asymmetries are significant — amplified negative + softened positive, or vice versa, are rhetorical configurations. The graduation envelope around key evaluations matters as much as the evaluations themselves.

**Engagement patterns**: Report the monogloss/heterogloss distribution. Map where the text speaks with categorical authority (monogloss) vs. where it hedges, attributes, or concedes (heterogloss expansion) vs. where it actively closes down alternatives (heterogloss contraction). The shift points — from heterogloss to monogloss, from attribute to endorse — are often where the most significant evaluative positioning happens.

**Synthesis**: Connect appraisal patterns to the text's positioning work. "The text reserves amplified positive Judgment (capacity) for [group A] and inscribed negative Judgment (propriety) with softened force for [group B], constructing an asymmetric evaluation that..." / "The text is monoglossic in its claims about X, treating them as beyond argument, while hedging its claims about Y — this distribution maps the text's epistemic authority claims onto its political commitments."

When used alongside fairclough-3d: Appraisal provides the detailed interpersonal/evaluative texture that maps onto Fairclough's Dimension 1 ideological work. Flag where the appraisal findings connect to the Dimension 3 social practice analysis.

When used alongside hyland-stance: Engagement overlaps significantly with Hyland's hedge/booster/shared-knowledge system, but Appraisal covers broader evaluative territory (Attitude, Graduation) and uses a more developed theoretical framework. Use Appraisal for depth; Hyland for comparison if academic/persuasive discourse conventions are also in scope.
