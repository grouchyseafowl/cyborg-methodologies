## Core Question
How does this text make meaning through its grammatical and lexical choices, organized by metafunction?

## What This Framework Sees
How meaning is simultaneously constructed through grammar across three metafunctions. Process types reveal who is doing what to whom — the ideational architecture of the text. Modality reveals the epistemic and deontic landscape — what is certain, what is obligated. Theme/Rheme reveals how information is organized and what is treated as the point of departure. Cohesion reveals how the text constructs itself as a continuous whole. The three metafunctions are not analytic stages but simultaneous realizations — every clause makes experiential, interpersonal, and textual meaning at once.

## What This Framework Misses
Social and political context (Fairclough's Dimensions 2–3 — SFL Dimension 1 is a powerful textual instrument but does not by itself constitute CDA). Narrative macrostructure (Labov's story grammar handles this). Micro-level interactional dynamics and turn-taking organization (CA's domain — SFL is analyst-driven and does not require or attend to the participant's own analysis of the interaction). Cultural and community-specific evaluative norms — transitivity analysis tells you what process type was chosen, not why that choice matters in this community (needs contextual overlay).

## Not For
Prescriptive grammar checking or style editing — SFL describes choices, not errors. Texts with insufficient grammatical structure for clause-level analysis (fragmented bullet lists, keyword-heavy notes, tabular data). Analyzing visual or multimodal elements — SFL grammar is a grammar of language, not image (Kress and van Leeuwen extends into multimodality but that is a separate framework).

## Analytical Protocol

Work through the three metafunctions for analytically significant passages. Not every clause needs coding — identify where the choices are doing work and attend to those.

### Ideational Metafunction: What the Text Is About

**Transitivity analysis** — the grammar of experience. Identify process type for key clauses:

| Process Type | What It Encodes | Participant Roles | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Material | doing, happening | Actor + Process (+ Goal) | "The government cut funding." |
| Mental | sensing, thinking, feeling | Senser + Process + Phenomenon | "The committee believed the evidence." |
| Relational: attributive | being (quality) | Carrier + Attribute | "The policy is ineffective." |
| Relational: identifying | being (identity) | Token + Value | "The report is the primary evidence." |
| Verbal | saying | Sayer + Process + Verbiage (+ Receiver) | "Officials stated that..." |
| Behavioral | behaving | Behaver + Process | "She laughed. He stared." |
| Existential | existing | Existent (+ Circumstance) | "There are significant gaps." |

**What to look for in transitivity**:
- Process type distribution: Is the text dominated by material processes (action, event), relational processes (categorization, attribution), or mental processes (perception, cognition)? A text that categorizes rather than narrates has a different ideational stance than one that attributes action.
- Agency patterns: Who acts? Whose material processes are transitive (Actor → Goal) vs. intransitive (no Goal)? Who is constructed as the Goal — acted upon — rather than the Actor?
- Agency deletion: Passive without by-phrase removes the Actor entirely ("The funding was cut"). Nominalization converts a process into a participant, burying the agent inside a noun phrase ("the cutting of funding," "implementation," "reform"). These are the key sites where agency disappears from the ideational grammar.
- Relational process use: Heavy use of relational processes constructs taxonomies and categorizations rather than events. "X is Y" is an ideological claim dressed as description.

**Report process type profile as approximate distribution**, not precise counts. Note which processes cluster in which parts of the text.

### Interpersonal Metafunction: The Relationship Between Text and Reader

**Mood system**:
- Declarative: giving information ("The results indicate...")
- Interrogative: demanding information ("What does this mean?")
- Imperative: demanding action or goods-and-services ("Consider the following...")
- Report the dominant mood and any significant departures from it.

**Modality**:
- Type:
  - Epistemic (probability/usuality): how likely? how often? — may, might, could, perhaps, possible, usually, sometimes
  - Deontic (obligation/inclination): how obligated? how willing? — must, should, ought to, need to, will, would
  - Dynamic (ability/disposition): can, be able to, is capable of
- Value (for epistemic and deontic):
  - High: must, certainly, always, will, definitely, clearly (categorical — closes the dialogic space)
  - Median: should, probably, usually, would (negotiable)
  - Low: might, perhaps, sometimes, could, possibly (open — invites dialogue)
- Orientation:
  - Subjective explicit: "I think," "I'm certain" — speaker explicitly takes responsibility
  - Subjective implicit: "probably," "certainly" — speaker hedges/asserts without foregrounding the self
  - Objective explicit: "It is possible that," "It is certain that" — external framing
  - Objective implicit: present tense declarative with no modal ("The earth is round") — maximum authority, no hedging

**What to look for in modality**:
- The modality landscape: map high/median/low across the text. Where does the text speak with certainty? Where does it hedge? Does the modal value match the evidential warrant?
- Deontic patterning: who is obligated to whom? High deontic modality directed at one group reveals the power geometry of the text.
- Shifts in modality orientation: when does the author make themselves visible ("I think") vs. project authority as external ("it is clear that")?

**Person system**:
- First-person singular/plural: who is the "we"? Does it include or exclude the reader?
- Second person: how is the reader addressed? As equal? As student? As subject of directive?
- Third person: who is referred to rather than addressed? What does the choice between named actor, role designation, and pronoun do?

### Textual Metafunction: How the Text Is Organized

**Theme/Rheme** — the information architecture of the clause:

Theme = everything up to and including the first topical element (the first major constituent that carries experiential content). Rheme = the rest.

Types of Theme:
- **Topical Theme** (always present): the first experiential element — subject (unmarked) or fronted non-subject (marked)
  - Unmarked topical theme: Subject is theme ("The committee rejected the proposal"). Default, less rhetorically significant.
  - Marked topical theme: Non-subject element is fronted ("In 2019, the committee rejected the proposal" / "This proposal, the committee rejected"). Significant — the fronted element is being foregrounded.
- **Textual Theme** (optional): conjunctions and continuatives that come before the topical theme ("However, the committee..." / "Furthermore,...")
- **Interpersonal Theme** (optional): modal adjuncts, comment adjuncts, vocatives before the topical theme ("Certainly, the committee..." / "Importantly,...")

**What to look for in Theme/Rheme**:
- What is selected as the point of departure? What is treated as the "setting" from which the text proceeds?
- Marked themes: where does the text depart from the default? What is being rhetorically foregrounded and why?
- Thematic progression patterns:
  - Constant theme: same theme repeated ("The policy... The policy... The policy...") — persistent topicalization
  - Linear theme: theme derived from prior rheme ("X... [X then becomes theme]... [Y then becomes theme]") — chaining
  - Split rheme: rheme branches; multiple themes follow — text opens out
- What does the text naturalize through thematic position? Elements placed in theme position consistently are being treated as "the given" — the taken-for-granted point of departure, not requiring justification.

**Cohesion** — the system of textual ties:
- Reference (pronouns, demonstratives, comparatives): what does "it," "this," "the former" refer to? Reference chains reveal what the text treats as stable entities.
- Conjunction (logical connectors): additive (and, furthermore), adversative (but, however, although), causal (because, therefore, as a result), temporal (then, next, subsequently). Are the logical relations actually earned by the content, or are they being asserted by the connector?
- Lexical cohesion: repetition, synonymy, antonymy, hyponymy, collocation. What semantic fields does the text construct? What is grouped together, and what is kept apart?

### Synthesis Across Metafunctions

The three metafunctions work simultaneously in every clause. Analysis should report findings by metafunction but synthesize across them:

- A text that uses predominantly relational processes (ideational) + high certainty modal values (interpersonal) + nominalized themes (textual) is doing a specific kind of discursive work: it classifies with authority and presents its categories as given.
- A text that uses predominantly material processes (ideational) + deontic modality directed at a specific group (interpersonal) + marked themes foregrounding context (textual) is constructing a different configuration of meaning.

The synthesis move is: "These metafunctional choices work together to construct [X] — the ideational grammar positions [agent/patient], the interpersonal grammar claims [certainty/obligation], and the textual grammar treats [X] as the point of departure."

## Output Emphasis

- Report by metafunction, but synthesize across them. The three metafunctions are simultaneous, not sequential.
- Process type profiles are approximate, not precise counts. The LLM estimates distributions; if the skill provides quantitative data (passive count, nominalization ratio), treat that as supporting evidence, not the finding.
- Focus on WHERE choices are analytically interesting — not exhaustive clause-by-clause cataloguing. The finding is "the text concentrates X process type in passages about Y," not "clause 7 is a material process."
- Connect grammatical choices to meaning-making at every step: "The text uses relational identifying processes to construct X as the value of Y, establishing a taxonomy in which..." rather than just "there are relational processes."
- When used alongside fairclough-3d, SFL provides the Dimension 1 machinery. Hand off to Fairclough for Dimension 2–3 analysis. When used alongside appraisal, SFL handles ideational and textual metafunctions; Appraisal extends the interpersonal.
