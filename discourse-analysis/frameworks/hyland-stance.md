## Core Question
How does this writer position themselves and engage their readers through interactive and interactional resources?

## What This Framework Sees
How writers construct their authorial persona, manage epistemic claims, and draw readers into the argument through a set of metadiscursive resources. Particularly powerful for academic, professional, and persuasive texts where the writer-reader relationship is central to the text's function. Reveals the rhetorical texture of argument — not just what is claimed but how claims are presented, qualified, emphasized, and the reader positioned relative to them. The hedge-to-booster ratio and its distribution across the text reveals the epistemic architecture of the argument: where the writer is confident, where they are negotiating, and whether that distribution maps onto the actual evidential warrant.

## What This Framework Misses
Non-textual social context (Fairclough Dimension 3 — Hyland focuses on the writer-reader dyad and the text's internal rhetorical management; power structures beyond that dyad require a different framework). Narrative macrostructure (Labov). Full attitudinal range — Appraisal covers Affect, Judgment, and Appreciation; Hyland's attitude markers are a subset of this territory. Power dynamics beyond the writer-reader relationship: who the writer is, who the reader is, and what structural conditions shape the interaction — these are external to the Hyland model.

## Not For
Texts without a clear writer-reader orientation: naturally occurring conversation transcripts, found institutional boilerplate written by committee without authorial voice, regulatory language. Texts where the analytically interesting discourse work is not about claim management or persuasion — where the question is not "how does the writer position themselves?" but "what social relations does this text help reproduce?" Use Fairclough for the latter. Texts where hedging is entirely absent because they are not making claims at all (procedural instructions, lists, tables).

## Analytical Protocol

Work through both systems (Stance, Engagement) for the full text, then map the distribution across text sections. The distribution matters as much as the inventory.

### System 1: STANCE — The Writer's Textual Voice

Stance resources are the devices through which the writer presents themselves in relation to their propositions. Four sub-systems:

#### Hedges: Withholding Full Commitment to a Proposition

Hedges mark claims as provisional, probabilistic, or based on reasoning rather than certainty. They open dialogic space — the reader is implicitly invited to assess the reasoning.

**Types**:
- Epistemic modals: may, might, could, would (in epistemic sense)
- Epistemic adverbs: perhaps, possibly, probably, apparently, seemingly
- Approximate quantifiers: about, approximately, roughly, around, nearly
- Mental process verbs (hedged): suggest, indicate, appear, seem, imply, tend to, it is possible that
- Attributive hedges: according to X, it has been suggested, some have argued (note overlap with Engagement-Attribute)

**Function**: Mark the writer's epistemic position as non-categorical. The hedge says: "this is my best current assessment, based on my reasoning from the evidence — it is not beyond question." In academic discourse, appropriate hedging is a marker of epistemic responsibility; over-hedging on well-supported claims signals low confidence or genre anxiety.

**Coding note**: distinguish hedges that soften epistemic commitment ("The results suggest...") from hedges that soften social face threat ("We might want to consider..."). The latter is politeness, not epistemics.

#### Boosters: Emphasizing Certainty or Closing Down Alternatives

Boosters mark claims as beyond question, shared, or fully warranted. They close dialogic space.

**Types**:
- Certainty adverbs: clearly, obviously, certainly, definitely, undoubtedly, of course, without question
- Emphatic adverbs: indeed, actually, really, in fact, truly
- Categorical frequency: always, never, invariably, consistently
- Categorical assertion verbs: demonstrate, prove, establish, confirm, show (vs. hedged suggest, indicate)

**Function**: The booster says: "this is not a matter of debate — I am asserting it as shared, warranted, or beyond question." In academic discourse, boosters signal the writer's confidence and implicitly challenge the reader to find grounds for disagreement. Overuse on weakly supported claims is an epistemic red flag. Strategic use on genuinely well-evidenced claims constructs appropriate authority.

**Key contrast**: "The data suggest X" (hedge — epistemic responsibility, dialogic) vs. "The data demonstrate X" (booster via verb choice — closed, authoritative). The choice between suggest and demonstrate is a significant stance resource.

#### Attitude Markers: Expressing the Writer's Affective Stance

Attitude markers indicate the writer's evaluation of the significance, value, or appropriateness of what they're discussing — distinct from hedges (epistemic) and boosters (certainty).

**Types**:
- Evaluative adjectives/adverbs on content: important, interesting, remarkable, surprising, unfortunate, troubling, significant, crucial
- Deontic stance: should, must, need to, it is essential/vital/critical/necessary that
- Evaluative comment clauses: "Importantly," "Significantly," "Crucially," "Surprisingly,"
- Affect markers on content: "Regrettably," "Unfortunately," "Tellingly,"

**Function**: Marks the writer's position on whether something matters, how it should be valued, or what response is appropriate. Deontic attitude markers make normative claims — they tell the reader what ought to be done or attended to, constructing the writer as someone with evaluative authority over the topic.

**Note**: Attitude markers overlap significantly with Appraisal's Attitude system. Hyland's framework provides a more streamlined analytical category for academic discourse contexts; Appraisal provides finer-grained sub-typing (Affect vs. Judgment vs. Appreciation) for texts where that distinction is analytically significant.

#### Self-Mention: Authorial Visibility

**Markers**: I, we, my, our, the author(s), the researcher(s), the present study, this paper.

**Function**: the degree to which the writer makes themselves explicitly present in the text. This is not simply a style preference — it is an epistemic and rhetorical positioning device.

- Heavy self-mention = claiming personal authority and taking responsibility for the argument ("I argue," "I contend," "I found")
- Moderate self-mention = constructing an engaged but professionally positioned author ("We analyzed," "Our approach")
- Minimal or absent self-mention = constructing objectivity, institutional voice, or impersonal authority ("The analysis reveals," "It was found," "Results show") — the knowledge appears to emerge from the data or the field rather than from the writer's reasoning

**Pattern to look for**: where self-mention concentrates in the text. Heavy self-mention in argument sections + absence in method sections is a common academic pattern (taking responsibility for interpretation, not for procedure). The reversal — self-mention in methods, impersonal in results — constructs a different epistemic persona. Absence of self-mention throughout in a contentious argument may signal that the writer is projecting authority through impersonality rather than earning it through explicit reasoning.

**First-person plural**: "We" in single-authored academic text often signals inclusive reader engagement ("As we can see...") rather than self-reference. Distinguish: is the "we" the author(s), or is it the author + reader as co-investigators?

### System 2: ENGAGEMENT — Drawing the Reader In

Engagement resources are the devices through which the writer constructs the reader as a participant and manages the writer-reader relationship within the text.

#### Reader Pronouns: Directly Addressing the Reader

**Markers**: you, your, one, we (inclusive — reader included), us.

**Function**: explicitly position the reader as a participant in the discourse. "As we can see from Figure 1" — the reader is made a co-analyst. "You will note that..." — the reader is being guided. "One might object that..." — a reader position is being constructed and managed.

**Note**: the inclusive "we" is both a self-mention marker and an engagement device when it includes the reader. Track whether "we" is author-only, author + reader, or disciplinary community.

#### Directives: Guiding the Reader's Attention

**Markers**: imperatives and deontic obligation directed at the reader. "Consider," "note that," "see Table 1," "recall that," "observe," "it should be noted that," "it is important to recognize."

**Function**: the writer directs the reader's cognitive attention — what to look at, what to remember, what to notice. Directives construct the writer as guide and the reader as someone being led through the argument. The density and tone of directives reveals the assumed reader-writer relationship: peer guidance ("Note that...") vs. instructional authority ("It must be recognized that...").

#### Questions: Real and Rhetorical

**Types**:
- Rhetorical questions: questions to which the text immediately provides the answer, or where the answer is assumed to be obvious. "Is this what we want from our institutions? Clearly not." — creates dialogic simulation while closing the dialogic space.
- Real questions: questions posed to open genuine inquiry, often at the beginning of sections. "What explains this pattern?" — may function as a genuine question (answer to follow) or as a framing device.

**Function**: questions create — or simulate — dialogic space. They invite the reader into the argument's movement rather than presenting conclusions. Rhetorical questions in particular often function as boosters: they assert the writer's position through the form of a question, constructing the answer as so obvious that only the question needs to be asked.

**Pattern to look for**: rhetorical questions that close down dialogic space while performing openness — they simulate engagement while functioning as contraction. This is a particularly common move in polemical academic writing.

#### Shared Knowledge Appeals: Constructing Solidarity Through the Given

**Markers**: "As is well known," "It is generally accepted that," "of course," "naturally," "as everyone knows," "it is a truism that," "researchers agree that," "the literature confirms."

**Function**: construct solidarity with the reader through the claim of shared knowledge. These markers say: "you and I, as members of this community, already know this — I don't need to argue for it." They simultaneously establish common ground and define the community of competent readers (those who share the knowledge) against implicit outsiders (those who don't).

**Note**: shared knowledge appeals overlap with Engagement's Proclaim-concur in Appraisal — they close dialogic space by presenting propositions as already beyond argument. They can function as boosters (asserting certainty through assumed community agreement) when the "shared knowledge" is actually contestable.

**Flag**: shared knowledge appeals that invoke agreement where the claim is in fact contested are significant rhetorical moves — they suppress potential objections by framing them as ignorance rather than legitimate disagreement.

#### Personal Asides: Authorial Persona Through Parenthetical Commentary

**Markers**: parenthetical remarks, em-dash insertions, footnote digressions that step outside the main argument to comment on it. "—and this is, I think, the most significant finding—" / "(I say 'roughly' because...)" / "as I noted at the outset."

**Function**: construct an authorial persona that is approachable, reflective, or self-aware. The aside says: "there is a writer here, thinking — not just a text." Heavy use of asides humanizes the academic persona; absence of asides contributes to impersonal authority construction.

### Hedge-to-Booster Ratio: The Epistemic Architecture of the Argument

The ratio of hedges to boosters across the text, and its distribution, is the single most analytically significant metric in Hyland's framework.

**Overall ratio**: is the text predominantly hedging (tentative, negotiable) or predominantly boosting (assertive, closed)?

**Distribution across text sections**: where does hedging concentrate? Where does boosting concentrate? Common patterns in academic writing:
- Hedging in literature review + boosting in contribution claims: "Prior work suggests X; this paper demonstrates Y." Constructs a field of uncertainty that the current work resolves.
- Hedging in results + boosting in method: "The method is established; the findings are preliminary." Epistemic caution where the evidence is weakest.
- Uniform boosting across all sections: high confidence claim regardless of evidential warrant — potential red flag.
- Hedging in argument + boosting in framing: the overarching frame is presented as beyond question; the specific claims are negotiable.

**Mismatch as finding**: when the hedge-to-booster distribution does NOT map onto the evidential strength of the claims — boosting on thin evidence, hedging on well-supported findings — the mismatch is itself analytically significant. What explains it? Genre convention? Uncertainty about authority to make the claim? Strategic rhetoric?

## Output Emphasis

- Report the hedge/booster distribution across the text, with attention to where each concentrates. The pattern, not the inventory, is the finding.
- Report self-mention patterns — where is the writer visible? Where absent? Does the visibility pattern shift across sections, and does it map onto the epistemic structure of the argument?
- Report engagement strategies — how is the reader positioned? As peer, as student, as audience? Do the reader-positioning devices construct the same reader relationship throughout, or does it shift?
- Connect stance patterns to the text's argumentative work: "The writer hedges most heavily in the discussion section [specific claims], while boosting methodological claims throughout, constructing certainty about how the research was done but epistemic caution about what it means."
- Note when hedging may reflect (a) genuine epistemic uncertainty, (b) genre convention (academic discourse norms), or (c) uncertainty about the writer's authority to make the claim — these have different analytical significance and different implications for the text's rhetorical strategy.
- When used alongside appraisal: flag where the systems overlap (Engagement-Attribute vs. Appraisal-Attribute; Hyland attitude markers vs. Appraisal Attitude) and where one provides more granularity. Use Appraisal for the full evaluative system; Hyland for the writer-reader relationship and claim management in academic/persuasive contexts.
- When used alongside fairclough-3d: Hyland's stance resources are Dimension 1 material, but they do not by themselves constitute Dimension 3 analysis. The question of why the writer hedges here and boosts there — whose interests this serves, what social positions it constructs — belongs to Fairclough's social practice dimension.
